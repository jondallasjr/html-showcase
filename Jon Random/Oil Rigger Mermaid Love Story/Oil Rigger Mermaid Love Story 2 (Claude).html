Act I: Descent

Where a storm changes two worlds forever

Meet Rafi "Rook" Navarro—28 years old, Filipino-American, and a poet of bolt sizes. He's union-curious and carries guilt from a small spill that weighs heavier than any wrench in his tool belt.
RAFI
"Every bolt tells a story. Every turn of the wrench... it matters. We're not just extracting oil—we're extracting pieces of the ocean's soul."
The storm arrived like an uninvited truth—sudden, powerful, and impossible to ignore. The rig, humanity's defiant monument to extraction, suddenly seemed very small against the ocean's fury.
OLD SALT (WHALE THOUGHTS)
[The surface-dwellers never learn. The sea always collects what it's owed.]
In the crushing darkness, salvation arrives wearing barnacles and ancient scars. Old Salt, a sperm whale whose songs carry the memory of migrations older than human industry, makes a choice that will change everything.

[Another surface-dweller falls from their metal island. But this one... this one feels different.]
Inside Old Salt's belly lies a world that defies surface logic—a cathedral of ribs and bioluminescent wonder, where air pockets form chambers of impossible sanctuary, and driftwood shrines honor boats lost to the deep.

Act II: Courtship & Friction

Learning each other's languages of love and loss

Language blooms between them in patterns of light and gesture. Mira weaves bioluminescent glyphs while Rafi offers the poetry of his tools—each bolt and wrench a word in the vocabulary of connection.

⟡⟡⟡ (Light-glyph pointing to his tool belt, creating a matching symbol)

Mira's "museum" reveals her fascination with surface dwellers—every item catalogued, every piece a mystery to decode. Teapots, tools, and debris become artifacts of a world she's never seen but desperately wants to understand.
Aunt Kelpie, Mira's pragmatic matriarch, prefers treaties to romance and politics to poetry. She's seen surface dwellers before—they take more than they give, pollute more than they preserve.
AUNT KELPIE
"Child, you study their artifacts like love letters. But remember—the surface world sends us their waste, not their gifts."
A simple gesture becomes a sacred promise. In offering his bolt as a gift, Rafi unknowingly participates in an ancient mer-marriage ritual. Love blooms in bioluminescent fireworks as two hearts accidentally bind themselves together.

Tessellate—the mer-city where law is sung and architecture grows from living coral. A world that thrives on connection rather than extraction, where every building breathes with the rhythm of the tides.
The surface world intrudes with violence disguised as progress. Seismic surveys tear through the ocean's song, disrupting the ancient whale communications and threatening the sanctuary that protects their love.

Act III: Reckoning & Grand Gesture

When love demands courage, and courage demands sacrifice

Old Salt, the ancient navigator whose scars tell stories of migrations older than human memory, makes the ultimate sacrifice—beaching himself to protect his pod from the surface world's acoustic violence.
OLD SALT
[Some anchors hold against storms. Others hold against forgetting. I choose to anchor memory in sand rather than let my family drown in noise.]
When love teaches you to listen, silence becomes the loudest protest. The rig workers lay down their tools not in abandonment, but in understanding—choosing the ocean's voice over extraction's profit.
RAFI (to the union)
"We've been writing the ocean's obituary with every shift. But what if instead we learned to read its poetry?"
In the end, love becomes a choice renewed with each breath, each tide, each migration. Two rings hang from Old Salt's rib—one forged from surface industry, one woven from ocean memory. Together, they sway with the rhythm of a whale's eternal heartbeat.
MIRA
"The surface and the deep don't have to be enemies. They can be... seasons of the same love."
RAFI
"Then let's learn to breathe in both worlds—with tools that build instead of take, with songs that heal instead of harm."
