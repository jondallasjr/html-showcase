The Algorithm That Cut Off Care

A reasonable-sounding business decision that harmed millions.

In 2019, researchers discovered that a healthcare algorithm used by major US hospitals and insurers to predict which patients needed extra care had a massive blind spot. The system scored approximately 200 million patients per year.

The algorithm used healthcare costs as a proxy for health needs. On the surface, that sounds reasonable: sicker people cost more to treat. But there was a problem built into the assumption.

The hidden bias: The US healthcare system historically spends less on Black patients — even when they are equally or more sick. So the algorithm concluded Black patients were healthier than they actually were.

The fix was straightforward: use direct health indicators (chronic conditions, lab results) instead of cost as a proxy. The bias was discoverable through demographic auditing — the problem was that nobody checked before deployment.

What Is AI Bias?

The 60-second version for busy leaders.

AI bias occurs when an AI system produces systematically unfair outcomes that privilege certain groups over others. It isn't a bug in the code. It's a failure in system design — from data collection through deployment.

The core insight: AI doesn't create bias. It scales existing bias at machine speed. An algorithm trained on historical data learns to replicate historical discrimination — and does it thousands of times per second, without questioning its own logic.

Quick Check: A bank uses an AI model to approve loans. The model doesn't include race as an input variable. Can it still be racially biased?

Where AI Bias Hides

Four categories. Dozens of ways in. Each one preventable.

Data Bias — What We Choose to Learn From

Historical bias: Training data reflects past inequality. Amazon's AI recruiting tool was trained on 10 years of resumes — overwhelmingly male. It learned to penalize resumes containing "women's" (e.g., "women's chess club captain") and downgrade all-women's college graduates.

Representation bias: Certain groups are under-sampled. The Gender Shades study found facial recognition had a 34% error rate for darker-skinned women vs. 1% for light-skinned men. Across 21 skin cancer datasets with 106,950 images, only 10 showed brown skin and 1 showed dark skin.

Measurement bias: Proxy variables don't capture what they claim to. Healthcare costs ≠ healthcare needs. The Optum algorithm is the textbook example.

Algorithmic Bias — What We Optimize For

Optimization bias: Facebook's ad delivery showed technical and executive job ads predominantly to men, even with gender-neutral targeting. The algorithm optimized for engagement and learned that men were more likely to click.

Feedback loops: Biased outputs reinforce themselves. Spotify's Discover Weekly recommended more male artists; user engagement with those recommendations made the system even more male-skewed over time.

Amplification: DALL-E 2 generated images of "CEO" as white men 97% of the time. GPT-3 associated Muslims with terrorism in 66% of sentence completions. AI doesn't just mirror society's biases — it exaggerates them.

Evaluation Bias — What We Pretend Is Working

Benchmark bias: Facial recognition showed 95%+ accuracy on the LFW benchmark — dominated by white male faces. NIST's diverse audit revealed massive racial and gender error gaps that the benchmark hid.

Metric blindness: Apple Card gave women lower credit limits than men with similar financial profiles. The overall model accuracy looked fine. But nobody measured for gender fairness until a viral tweet exposed the problem.

Human Bias — What We Normalize

Automation bias: People tend to trust automated systems over their own judgment, regardless of error rates. When a model says "reject this applicant," reviewers rarely override it — even when something feels wrong.

Confirmation bias: Teams favor results that confirm existing beliefs. If your fraud detection model mostly flags transactions from certain communities, the team sees "evidence" that those communities are riskier — when it might just be biased detection.

Key insight: Proxy discrimination is the #1 mechanism. Algorithms rarely use race, age, or gender directly. Instead, they use variables — healthcare costs, ZIP codes, resume keywords — that correlate with protected characteristics and reproduce the same discriminatory outcomes.

When Bias Hits the Bottom Line

Four real cases. Real lawsuits. Real settlements. Real regulatory action.

Hiring

Mobley v. Workday (2025)

A Black applicant over 40 with a disability applied to 80–100 positions via Workday's AI screening. Rejected by nearly all without an interview.

What happened: Workday's algorithm compared applicant profiles to job requirements and generated hiring recommendations. Despite being qualified, Mobley was systematically rejected.

Consequence: A federal judge granted preliminary class-action certification — the first major case establishing that AI vendors (not just employers) can be liable under anti-discrimination law.

What should have been done: Regular bias audits across protected categories. Human review for applicants rejected by automated systems. Adverse impact testing before deployment.

Lending

Algorithmic Mortgage Discrimination

AI lending algorithms charged Black and Latinx borrowers more — even with identical credit profiles.

What happened: A Lehigh University study found Black applicants needed credit scores approximately 120 points higher than white applicants for the same approval rate. A UC Berkeley study found fintech algorithms charged nearly 5 basis points more in interest.

Consequence: An estimated $450 million per year in excess charges extracted from minority borrowers. The CFPB now requires lenders using AI to provide specific, accurate denial reasons.

What should have been done: Audit algorithms for disparate impact across racial groups. Avoid proxy variables (ZIP code, educational institution) that correlate with race. The Lehigh study found that explicitly instructing the AI to "use no bias" nearly eliminated disparities.

Housing

SafeRent Tenant Screening

AI "SafeRent Score" disproportionately flagged Black and Hispanic rental applicants as high-risk.

What happened: SafeRent Solutions marketed an AI score that claimed to "automate human judgment" in tenant screening. The algorithm produced scores that disproportionately denied housing to Black and Hispanic applicants with similar financial profiles.

Consequence: SafeRent paid more than $2 million to settle. The court held that AI vendors bear responsibility even when landlords make the final decision.

What should have been done: Test scoring outputs for disparate impact. Make criteria transparent to applicants. Provide appeal mechanisms. Recognize that "automating human judgment" can mean automating human biases.

Healthcare

Optum Risk Prediction

A cost-based algorithm systematically underestimated the health needs of Black patients.

What happened: The algorithm used healthcare spending as a proxy for health needs. Because the system historically spent less on Black patients, they were scored as healthier — even when they had more chronic conditions.

Consequence: Before recalibration, only 17.7% of patients flagged for extra care were Black. After switching to direct health indicators, that number rose to 46.5% — nearly tripling representation.

What should have been done: Use direct health measures instead of cost proxies. Audit outcomes by demographic group before deployment. Recognize that "neutral" variables can encode systemic inequality.

The legal trend is clear: Courts are increasingly holding that AI vendors — not just the companies deploying their tools — bear legal responsibility for discriminatory outcomes. If you build it, you own the bias.

You Are the Chief Risk Officer

Your company is deploying an AI credit scoring model. Make the right calls.

Step 1: The data science team wants to include ZIP code as a feature in the credit model. It's a strong predictor of default risk. What do you do?

Good call. ZIP code is a classic proxy variable. It correlates with race due to historical housing segregation. The right move is to flag it for disparate impact analysis — not blindly approve or blindly remove it.

Step 2: The model is built. Before deployment, how should you test it for bias?

Exactly right. The 80% rule (four-fifths rule) says the selection rate for any protected group should be at least 80% of the highest-performing group. Overall accuracy can mask demographic-level failures — you must measure subgroup outcomes.

Step 3: The bias audit reveals that Black applicants are denied at 1.8x the rate of white applicants with similar income levels. What's your response?

The right call. A 1.8x denial rate likely fails the four-fifths test. Deploying a known-biased model — even with a manual review layer — creates legal liability. The Workday and SafeRent cases show courts are holding vendors accountable.

Step 4: The retrained model passes the fairness audit. It's deployed. Six months later, you need to decide on ongoing monitoring. What do you implement?

That's the gold standard. Bias drifts over time as data distributions change. Continuous monitoring with automated alerts catches problems before they become lawsuits. Quarterly audits add an independent check. Waiting for annual reviews or complaints means learning about bias from a regulator or a headline.

Simulation complete. The decisions you just walked through map to a real bias audit framework: identify proxy variables → test with disparate impact analysis → remediate before deployment → monitor continuously. This is the process every enterprise deploying AI needs.

The Monday Morning Playbook

Six things your organization can start doing this week.

  1. 1
    Audit your data. Check training datasets for representativeness. Identify proxy variables (ZIP code, education, purchase history) that correlate with protected characteristics. Document data provenance.
  2. 2
    Measure fairness. Apply the 80% rule (four-fifths test): the selection rate for any demographic group should be at least 80% of the highest-performing group. Measure demographic parity, equalized odds, and predictive parity.
  3. 3
    Build diverse teams. Include ethicists, social scientists, domain experts, and community representatives alongside engineers. Members from affected communities identify bias issues first.
  4. 4
    Maintain human oversight. High-stakes decisions (hiring, lending, healthcare, housing) should never be fully automated. Define which decisions require human review and document the rationale.
  5. 5
    Monitor continuously. Bias drifts over time as data and behavior change. Set up automated alerts when fairness metrics fall below thresholds. Don't wait for annual reviews.
  6. 6
    Get independent audits. Internal teams have blind spots. Engage third-party auditors for critical systems. The EU AI Act requires this for high-risk AI.
