The Task Executioner vs Problem Solver Framework

Traditional job descriptions train you to be a task executioner. They list activities: "Manage stakeholder communications," "Prepare weekly reports," "Coordinate project meetings." This creates what I call "checkbox thinking"—you complete the tasks, tick the boxes, and wait for the next set of instructions.

Consultant-level thinking is fundamentally different. Instead of asking "What should I work on?" you ask "What problems need solving?"

The Mental Model Shift

Tasks are inputs. Problems are opportunities. Solutions are products.

Task executioners deliver inputs. Problem solvers deliver products. The market pays dramatically different rates for these two approaches.

The Problem Identification System

Most professionals wait for problems to be handed to them in job descriptions or project briefs. Consultants actively hunt for problems because that's where the value—and the money—lies.

After 17 years of transformation work, I've developed a systematic approach to problem identification that I call the "Consultant's Radar."

Level 1: Surface Problems +

These are the obvious issues people complain about. Inefficient processes, unclear communications, missed deadlines. Task executioners work on these because they're visible and well-defined.

Level 2: System Problems +

These are the underlying causes creating the surface problems. Poor stakeholder alignment, conflicting priorities, inadequate governance. This is where real consultants operate because fixing system problems eliminates multiple surface problems.

Level 3: Strategic Problems +

These are the fundamental misalignments between what the organization says it wants and what its structures actually deliver. Most people don't even see these problems, which is exactly why they're valuable to identify and solve.

When I identified that the Treasury's project approval process was creating delays worth millions in taxpayer costs, that wasn't in anyone's job description. But solving it saved £500 million.

The Framework

Stop asking "What should I do?" and start asking "What shouldn't be happening that is?" or "What should be happening that isn't?"

The Value Creation Mental Model

Job description thinking trains you to trade time for money. You work eight hours, you get paid for eight hours. There's a linear relationship between input and output. But consultant thinking is about creating value, not consuming time.

Here's the shift: Time is your cost. Value is your price.

The Mental Blocker Self-Check

Most people have invisible barriers preventing them from thinking beyond job descriptions. After working with hundreds of professionals making this transition, I've identified the most common mental blockers:

"I need more experience before I can position myself as a problem-solver." +

Reality check: Experience helps, but problem-solving ability matters more. Some of the most effective consultants I know started identifying problems and creating solutions in their first roles. They just positioned it differently than their peers.

"Someone else should be making these strategic decisions." +

This is hierarchy thinking. In consulting, value creation trumps organizational charts. If you can solve a problem, you're qualified to propose the solution—regardless of your title or tenure.

"I don't have enough information to suggest solutions." +

This is perfectionism disguised as prudence. Consultants work with incomplete information all the time. The skill isn't having perfect data—it's creating frameworks that work with available information and identifying what additional information would be valuable.

"What if my solution doesn't work?" +

Employee mindset fears being wrong. Consultant mindset expects iteration. The goal isn't perfect solutions—it's better solutions than the status quo.

The Immediate Action Framework

Here's how you start thinking like a consultant instead of an employee, starting tomorrow:

Week 1

Problem Spotting

Instead of focusing on your task list, spend 30 minutes each day identifying problems around you. What's inefficient? What's unclear? What's causing delays or frustration? Write them down.

Week 2

Root Cause Analysis

Take the problems you've identified and ask "Why is this happening?" Keep asking why until you get to system-level causes, not just surface symptoms.

Week 3

Solution Sketching

For each system-level problem, sketch 2-3 potential solutions. Don't worry about perfect implementation details—focus on the core approach.

Week 4

Value Articulation

For your best solutions, quantify the potential impact. Time saved, costs avoided, risks mitigated. This trains you to connect your thinking to business outcomes.
