The Trap You Don't Even Know You're In

When I was building transformation programs at HM Treasury, I watched hundreds of smart people get stuck in this mindset. They'd wait for someone to tell them what to work on. They'd ask for clearer job descriptions. They'd want approval before taking action. And they wondered why their careers plateaued while consultants like me were billing £1,500 a day.

Job descriptions are income ceilings disguised as career guidance.

Every time you think "that's not in my job description," you're reinforcing employee mindset. Every time you wait for someone else to define your priorities, you're positioning yourself as order-taker, not problem-solver. And every time you focus on tasks instead of outcomes, you're commoditizing yourself.

The Task Executioner vs Problem Solver Framework

Mental Model Shift:

Tasks are inputs. Problems are opportunities. Solutions are products.

Task executioners deliver inputs. Problem solvers deliver products. The market pays dramatically different rates for these two approaches.

Traditional job descriptions train you to be a task executioner. They list activities: "Manage stakeholder communications," "Prepare weekly reports," "Coordinate project meetings." This creates what I call "checkbox thinking"—you complete the tasks, tick the boxes, and wait for the next set of instructions.

Consultant-level thinking is fundamentally different. Instead of asking "What should I work on?" you ask "What problems need solving?" Instead of focusing on activities, you focus on outcomes. Instead of waiting for job descriptions, you write your own scope based on what you observe.

Real Example:
When I was working with the Cabinet Office on a major transformation, they initially wanted someone to "manage the project communications." That's task executioner thinking—£500 a day work. But I positioned it differently: "I'll ensure stakeholder alignment doesn't become a delivery risk." Same activity, different framing. The outcome focus commanded a premium rate because it addressed their real fear: project failure due to political complications.

The Problem Identification System

Most professionals wait for problems to be handed to them in job descriptions or project briefs. Consultants actively hunt for problems because that's where the value—and the money—lies.

The "Consultant's Radar" System:

Level 1: Surface Problems - The obvious issues people complain about. Inefficient processes, unclear communications, missed deadlines. Task executioners work on these because they're visible and well-defined.

Level 2: System Problems - The underlying causes creating the surface problems. Poor stakeholder alignment, conflicting priorities, inadequate governance. This is where real consultants operate.

Level 3: Strategic Problems - Fundamental misalignments between what the organization says it wants and what its structures actually deliver. Most people don't even see these problems, which is exactly why they're valuable.

The Solution Ownership Framework

Problem-Solution-Value Triangulation:
  1. Problem Articulation: Define the issue in business impact terms, not task terms
  2. Solution Architecture: Present options and recommendations, not just implementation
  3. Value Quantification: Connect your approach to measurable outcomes

Job descriptions create what I call "solution avoidance." They define problems narrowly and prescribe approaches specifically. This trains people to implement other people's thinking rather than develop their own solutions.

The person who owns the solution owns the value conversation.

This transforms you from someone who executes other people's plans to someone who creates plans worth executing.

The Mental Blocker Self-Check

Most people have invisible barriers preventing them from thinking beyond job descriptions. After working with hundreds of professionals making this transition, I've identified the most common mental blockers:

Mental Blocker Reality Check:

"I need more experience before I can position myself as a problem-solver."
Reality: Experience helps, but problem-solving ability matters more. Start identifying problems and creating solutions in your current role.
"Someone else should be making these strategic decisions."
This is hierarchy thinking. In consulting, value creation trumps organizational charts.
"I don't have enough information to suggest solutions."
This is perfectionism disguised as prudence. Consultants work with incomplete information all the time.
"What if my solution doesn't work?"
Employee mindset fears being wrong. Consultant mindset expects iteration.

The Immediate Action Framework

Here's how you start thinking like a consultant instead of an employee, starting tomorrow:

Week 1: Problem Spotting

  • Spend 30 minutes each day identifying problems around you
  • Ask: What's inefficient? What's unclear? What's causing delays?
  • Write them down. Don't try to solve them yet—just develop your problem radar

Week 2: Root Cause Analysis

  • Take identified problems and ask "Why is this happening?"
  • Keep asking why until you get to system-level causes
  • This trains your brain to think strategically instead of tactically

Week 3: Solution Sketching

  • For each system-level problem, sketch 2-3 potential solutions
  • Focus on the core approach, not perfect implementation details
  • This develops your solution architecture muscle

Week 4: Value Articulation

  • For your best solutions, quantify the potential impact
  • Calculate time saved, costs avoided, risks mitigated
  • This trains you to connect your thinking to business outcomes

After four weeks of this practice, you'll notice your conversations change. Instead of discussing tasks, you'll discuss problems. Instead of asking for instructions, you'll present options. Instead of waiting for job descriptions, you'll be creating scope.

Breaking Free From the Employee Trap

The job description mindset isn't just about career limitations—it's about how you see your role in creating value. When you define yourself by someone else's task list, you've outsourced your professional identity.

The market doesn't pay premium rates for task completion. It pays premium rates for problem elimination.

After building three consulting businesses and advising on programs worth over £10 billion, here's what I know: Every time you position yourself as a problem-solver instead of a task-executor, you move up the value chain. Every time you present solutions instead of asking for instructions, you demonstrate consultant-level thinking. Every time you focus on outcomes instead of activities, you justify higher rates.

The consulting industry has shifted dramatically toward outcome-based relationships. Clients want partners who solve complex problems, not contractors who execute predetermined tasks. Especially as AI handles more routine work, the premium is on strategic thinking and creative problem-solving.

Remember:

This isn't about changing careers—it's about changing how you think about the career you have. Whether you're employed or independent, whether you're junior or senior, whether you're in government or private sector, you can start thinking like a consultant.

The job description is just someone else's guess about what needs doing. Your value lies in figuring out what actually needs doing—and then doing it.
