SECTION 1: THE £1K+ MINDSET

You're Not Expensive Enough

Early in my consulting career, I sat across the table from a client who was paying me £450 a day. Good money, I thought. Better than a salary. I was doing solid work — restructuring their programme governance, cleaning up a mess that had been festering for months.

Then I found out the consultant sitting two desks over — doing the same level of work — was billing £1,500 a day.

Same client. Same building. Same type of work. Three times my rate.

He wasn't smarter than me. He didn't have better qualifications. He'd actually been consulting for fewer years. But he walked in differently. He spoke differently. He positioned himself differently.

That was the moment I realised: the gap between a £450/day consultant and a £1,500/day consultant has almost nothing to do with skill.

Most consultants think like employees. They price like contractors. They wait to be told what to do. And they wonder why they're stuck.

This lesson changes that. By the end of these eight pages, you'll see exactly why you've been leaving money on the table — and you'll have the mental framework to stop.

"The gap isn't skill. It's how you think. Change how you think, and the money follows."

THE GAP

Same Skills. Different Bank Accounts.

Let me paint you a picture. Two consultants walk into the same client organisation on the same Monday morning.

Consultant A — £400/day

  • Waits for a brief before starting
  • Asks "What do you want me to do?"
  • Tracks hours meticulously
  • Competes on availability
  • Sees themselves as hired help
  • Prices based on old salary + margin

Consultant B — £1,500/day

  • Diagnoses the problem before being asked
  • Says "Here's what you need to do"
  • Prices on outcomes, not hours
  • Competes on insight and authority
  • Sees themselves as a trusted advisor
  • Prices based on value delivered

Same qualifications. Same years of experience. Same client. The only difference is what's happening between their ears.

I lived this. I went from junior business analyst to billing £1,500 a day to founding a consultancy charging £4,000 a day. The work didn't change. I did.

One consultant increased their fees by 350% with identical deliverables — they only changed the pricing model. That's not a typo. Same work. Three and a half times the money.

"Clients don't pay for what you do. They pay for what you represent."

THE TRAP

The Mindset That's Costing You £100K/Year

Here's the uncomfortable truth. Most consultants don't have a skills problem. They have an identity problem.

They left employment but brought the employee mindset with them. And that mindset is costing them — conservatively — £100,000 a year in lost income.

The employee mindset is comfortable. Someone else defines success. Someone else takes the risk. Someone else decides what you're worth. And that comfort is the thing that's keeping you cheap.

Research shows that consultants who actively work on their mindset are 2.3x more likely to do weekly outreach to new clients. 65% versus 28%. The difference isn't hustle — it's how you see yourself.

Here's the worst part: failing to make this shift doesn't just limit your income. It recreates the boss-subordinate dynamic inside what should be an independent practice. You end up working at the client's whims, waiting for instructions, hoping they'll renew — exactly like the job you left.

REVEAL

The Three Tells That Mark You as "Hired Help"

Think about your own behaviour first — then click each card to reveal the tell.

1 The Question Tell ▼

You ask "What do you want me to do?" instead of diagnosing the problem yourself.

The moment you ask this question, you've positioned yourself as a pair of hands. An advisor doesn't ask for instructions. They assess the situation, form a view, and present it. "Based on what I've seen, here's what needs to happen." That's a fundamentally different relationship.

2 The Pricing Tell ▼

You price by the hour or day instead of by the outcome.

Hourly billing punishes efficiency. The faster you solve a problem, the less you earn. That's backwards. Premium consultants price on the value they deliver. If your solution saves a client £2 million, your fee is a rounding error — regardless of how many hours it took.

3 The Initiative Tell ▼

You wait to be invited instead of proactively identifying what needs fixing.

Employees wait for their next assignment. Advisors see problems before they're pointed out. When I walked into the Treasury, I didn't wait for someone to hand me a brief. I spent the first week observing, listening, and forming my own view of what was broken. By the time they asked for my assessment, I already had one.

Recognise any of these? Most consultants do. The good news is that once you can see the trap, you can walk out of it.

THE SHIFT

Think Like Their Most Trusted Advisor

There's a framework I keep coming back to. It was developed by David Maister, and it explains exactly why some consultants get invited back and others get replaced.

It's called the Trust Equation:

TRUST = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) ÷ Self-Orientation
The denominator is the killer. Your trustworthiness plummets the moment you prioritise your interests over the client's.

Most consultants focus on the top of the equation — credibility, track record, deliverables. But the thing that actually separates premium advisors from hired help is the denominator. Low self-orientation means your focus is on the client's success, not your next invoice.

The Reframe That Multiplies Fees

Here's a real example. A client says: "We need customer service training."

The employee-consultant quotes a training programme. Four figures. Done.

The advisor-consultant says: "Customer satisfaction needs to increase from 80% to 85%. That translates to roughly £1 million in retained revenue. Training is one lever among several organisational changes we should consider."

Same starting point. The first conversation leads to a four-figure contract. The second leads to a six-figure engagement.

The difference? The advisor reframed the problem, not just the solution.

"When I walked into the Treasury billing £2,000 a day, I wasn't there to take orders. I was there because they needed someone who could see what they couldn't."

There's a consultant named Betsy Jordyn who describes this perfectly. She identifies two traps most consultants fall into: the "pair of hands" trap (you implement whatever the client asks) and the "surrogate leader" trap (you do the leader's job for them). The sweet spot is being a "leader of process" — you own how decisions get made. The client owns the decisions.

Her line that stuck with me: "He who has the marker controls the world."

THE MATHS

Why Clients Happily Pay £1,000/Day

Here's something most consultants never think about: hourly billing punishes efficiency.

The faster you solve a problem, the less you earn. That's not just backwards — it creates an adversarial relationship. The client starts scrutinising your timesheets. You start padding your hours. Nobody wins.

The Maths That Changes Everything

A consultant billing £300/hour spends 10 hours on a project. That's £3,000.

That project saves the client £200,000.

A value-based fee of 20% on that outcome? £40,000.

Same work. Same 10 hours. Thirteen times the money.

The psychology works in your favour too. 95% of buying decisions are subconscious. Clients use price as a shortcut for quality. A higher fee doesn't just earn you more — it makes the client more committed to the engagement. They invest more effort. They follow through on recommendations. They get better results.

Charging more literally makes your clients better clients.

"The framework that saved taxpayers £500 million. I didn't bill for my time. I billed for the outcome."

Here's the ROI framework I use: your fee should deliver 3-10x return for the client. If your strategy generates £500,000 in annual value, a £50,000 fee is an easy decision for any executive with a calculator.

Think about your last project. What was the business impact in pounds? Now divide your fee into that number. What's the ratio?

If your ratio is above 10:1, you're dramatically underpriced. Most consultants I coach find ratios of 20:1 or 30:1 when they actually do this exercise. That means the client is getting 20-30x return on what they pay you. You're not expensive — you're a bargain. And the client probably knows it, even if you don't.

Start tracking this number on every engagement. It's your ammunition for the pricing conversation.

PUT IT INTO PRACTICE

What Would You Do?

Time to test yourself. Here are two scenarios you'll face as a consultant. There's no trick — just choose the response closest to what you'd actually do. Then see what happens.

Scenario 1: The Kickoff Meeting

You're in a kickoff meeting. The client slides a detailed brief across the table and says: "Can you deliver this by Friday?"

Employee move. You just accepted the client's framing without question. You're now a pair of hands executing someone else's plan. Even if you deliver brilliantly, you've capped yourself at task-executor status. The client will see you as interchangeable — because you acted like it.
Better, but still reactive. You're buying time, which is smart. But you're still operating inside the client's frame. You haven't challenged whether the brief itself is the right approach. You're a thoughtful employee, not an advisor.
Advisor move. You've just done three things. You've shown you think beyond the task. You've signalled that you care about outcomes, not just deliverables. And you've positioned yourself as someone who challenges assumptions. This is how £1,500/day consultants operate from minute one.

Scenario 2: The Challenge

You've presented your recommendation to the steering committee. A senior stakeholder leans back, folds their arms, and says: "I'm not convinced this is the right approach. We tried something similar two years ago and it didn't work."

You just folded. The stakeholder tested your conviction and you retreated. You've signalled that your recommendation isn't something you believe in strongly enough to defend. Next time, they'll go around you — or replace you. Pushback isn't an attack. It's a test.
Decent instinct, wrong energy. You're showing curiosity, which matters. But asking the stakeholder to explain the past failure puts them in the driver's seat. You should already know what happened two years ago. An advisor does their homework.
That's how you hold the room. You acknowledged the concern without conceding. You showed you'd anticipated the objection. And you demonstrated depth of preparation. This is the moment where the room decides whether you're an advisor or a vendor. You just passed the test.

Here's something the research confirmed: 90% of senior consultants aren't hired for economic reasons — they're hired for political ones. Executives bring in premium advisors for stakeholder alignment and political navigation. The advisor sees the politics. The employee just sees the task.

THE INTERNAL GAME

The Voice That Keeps You Cheap

Everything I've taught you so far is useless if there's a voice in your head telling you it doesn't apply to you.

I know that voice. I've had it. Every consultant I've coached has had it. It sounds reasonable. It sounds like humility. But it's not — it's fear dressed up as logic.

Click the blockers you recognise in yourself. Be honest — I'll give you my response to each one.

01 "I don't have enough experience yet."

I started billing £1,000 a day before most of my competitors had half my years. Experience is a story you tell yourself. Clients don't buy experience — they buy confidence, insight, and the ability to solve their problem. I've seen five-year consultants outperform twenty-year ones because they thought like advisors from day one.

If you're good enough for the client to hire you, you're good enough to charge what you're worth.

02 "Clients won't pay that much."

A consultant named Heather Younger actually lost a project because her fees were too low. The client assumed low price meant low value. That's not an anecdote — it's how buying psychology works. 95% of buying decisions are subconscious, and price is a quality signal.

24% of consultants don't raise their rates because they fear losing clients. But the fear is almost always assumption-based, not evidence-based. Test it. You'll be surprised.

03 "I need more qualifications first."

I didn't get my biggest contracts because of certifications. I got them because I walked in thinking like an advisor, not an applicant. Qualifications are a comfort blanket. They make you feel ready. But clients don't check your certificates — they check your presence.

If you're waiting until you feel "qualified enough" to charge premium rates, you'll wait forever. That feeling doesn't come from certificates. It comes from deciding.

04 "It's easy for me, so it can't be worth that much."

This one is sneaky. You discount your work precisely because you're good at it. "While it may be easy for us, it's not easy for them. That's why they're coming to us." Your ease is your expertise. It took years to make it look effortless.

A brain surgeon doesn't charge less because the operation only took 40 minutes. They charge for the 15 years it took to make it 40 minutes.

05 "I should be grateful for any work."

The scarcity mindset. It sounds humble. It's not. It's fear. And it creates a vicious cycle: low-paying clients are often the most demanding. They drain your energy, fill your calendar, and block you from the premium opportunities you should be pursuing.

Here's what actually happens when you raise your rates: the difficult, price-sensitive clients leave. And you have space for the ones who value what you bring. Higher rates attract better clients. Better clients get better results. Better results justify even higher rates. The cycle works in your favour — but only if you start.
