They're revealing something much deeper about how they perceive value, and more importantly, giving you the exact roadmap to address their real concerns.

The Hidden Reality Behind "Too Expensive"

Here's what I've discovered through hundreds of high-stakes negotiations: if a prospect gives you a price objection, you simply haven't communicated enough value. But often, it's about something else altogether, and they simply use pricing as an easy way out[2]. Understanding this distinction is what separates £500-per-day consultants from those commanding £2,000-plus rates.

The data backs this up powerfully. Research shows that 75% of price objections are based on perceived rather than actual value gaps[3]. Think about what this means: three-quarters of the time when someone says you're too expensive, they're not actually objecting to your price—they're telling you they don't understand your value proposition clearly enough.

Six Categories of Hidden Objections

▼

The Whack Back: These buyers always ask for reductions because it's worked before. They've been conditioned to expect discounts and view the initial price as a starting point for negotiation.

The Bluffer: They have budget allocated but don't want you to know. They're testing your confidence and willingness to defend your value.

The Value Challenger: They genuinely don't see how your solution delivers measurable return on investment.

Each category requires a completely different tactical response, which is why generic price objection scripts fail miserably in real-world consulting scenarios.

Step One: The Strategic Pause

When you hear "that's too expensive" or any variation, your first instinct is to respond immediately. Don't. Instead, pause for exactly three to five seconds of complete silence[4]. This isn't dramatic effect—it's psychological leverage.

During those seconds, something powerful happens in the prospect's mind. The silence pushes them to elaborate on their reasons for objecting, giving you intelligence you can't get any other way[2]. More importantly, it prevents you from the fatal mistake of bidding against yourself by immediately offering a lower price.

I learned this the hard way during a Cabinet Office engagement. My immediate defensive response to a price objection revealed my own lack of confidence and undermined the value I'd spent weeks establishing. The silence technique forces prospects to reveal their real concerns while positioning you as someone who stands confidently behind their pricing.

Step Two: Intelligent Questioning

After the pause, you can ask up to three questions before responding to the objection[4]. This isn't interrogation—it's strategic intelligence gathering that transforms vague objections into specific concerns you can address.

The most powerful question I've discovered is deceptively simple: "Too expensive compared to what?" This immediately shifts the conversation from your pricing to their comparison framework, giving you the exact positioning battle you need to win.

Alternative Questions That Work:
"Can you help me understand what specifically about the investment concerns you?"
"What would need to be true about the outcomes for this to feel like excellent value?"
"Help me understand the cost of not solving this problem."

Each question serves a strategic purpose: uncovering their comparison standard, identifying specific value gaps, or quantifying the cost of inaction.

Step Three: Value Anchoring and Reframing

Here's where most consultants go wrong—they immediately start justifying their price rather than reinforcing the value that justifies it. The key is leveraging their reasons to emphasize the value of your business[2], not defending the cost of your time.

During a major transformation program, when the client expressed price concerns, I responded: "I understand the investment feels significant. Let me make sure I'm understanding correctly—you've identified that this inefficiency is costing £500,000 annually, and the board is expecting resolution within six months. Is that correct?"

This reframe accomplished three things simultaneously: acknowledged their concern, reinforced the problem's cost, and established the urgency timeline. By the time I finished, the conversation had shifted from my £150,000 fee being expensive to it representing a 3-to-1 return on investment.

The Formula

Value First + Problem Cost + Implementation Urgency = Price Justification

Step Four: Confident Price Defense

Never let someone tell you the price is too high and ask you to offer a lower price. This is bidding against yourself and destroys both margins and perceived value[5]. Instead, explain that price is inextricably linked to value—if they don't want to pay full price, they won't get full value[4].

My standard response has become: "I appreciate your concern about the investment. What I've learned after seventeen years and saving taxpayers over £500 million is that when we reduce the fee, we necessarily reduce the scope, timeline, or quality of outcomes. Which of those would you prefer to compromise?"

This response does several things: establishes credibility, reinforces value delivered to similar clients, explains the trade-off reality, and puts the choice back to them. Most importantly, it maintains your positioning as a premium provider who delivers proportional value.

The ROI Conversation Framework

The most powerful price objection response I've developed centers on return on investment calculation. If you provide £100,000 of value, charging £10,000-£20,000 represents exceptional ROI for the client[7]. The key is asking questions so clients tell YOU what the value is and agree to it, then communicating how your investment represents significant returns[7].

1

Problem Quantification

"Help me understand the annual cost of this challenge continuing unsolved."

2

Outcome Valuation

"What would successful resolution be worth to the organization annually?"

3

Timeline Urgency

"How quickly do you need to see these results?"

4

ROI Calculation

"So if we're talking about £500,000 in annual value, delivered within six months, how does a £75,000 investment compare?"

This approach transforms price objections into investment discussions, positioning your fee as a business decision with measurable returns rather than a cost center.

Real-World Implementation Templates

Based on hundreds of successful price defense conversations, here are the specific phrases and frameworks that consistently work in consulting scenarios:

For Value Clarification:
"I want to make sure we're aligned on the value being delivered. You've mentioned [specific problem] is costing [quantified impact]. My approach addresses this by [solution methodology], typically delivering [outcome range] within [timeline]. Does that match your understanding of what success looks like?"
For Comparison Reframing:
"Too expensive compared to what? If you're comparing to [lower-cost alternative], I should clarify the difference in scope and outcomes you can expect."
For Budget Reality Testing:
"Help me understand your budget expectations. I want to make sure we're designing a solution that matches the resources available and delivers proportional value."
For Urgency Reinforcement:
"What's the cost of waiting another [time period] to address this? Sometimes the expense of delay outweighs the investment in immediate resolution."

Each template serves as a starting point, but the key is adapting language to your specific situation while maintaining the psychological principles that make them effective.

The Confidence Factor

One of the most important elements in running a successful consulting business is mindset and confidence[8]. Your belief in your value directly impacts how prospects perceive and respond to your pricing.

After years of commanding premium rates, I've observed that consultants who immediately offer discounts or lengthy justifications communicate uncertainty about their own value. Conversely, those who respond to price objections with calm confidence and strategic questioning consistently close at higher rates.

The data supports this observation. Research shows that in all negotiations, both sides want to feel they're making a good deal. If you say "yes" too quickly, the other party will wonder if they made a bad deal and often try to add terms or make exceptions[8].

Your confidence in defending price translates directly to client confidence in your value. When you demonstrate unwavering belief in your worth through tactical objection handling, prospects interpret this as evidence of the exceptional outcomes you'll deliver.

Implementation Action Plan

Mastering price objection handling requires practice with real scenarios. Here's your immediate implementation framework:

1

Practice Phase

Role-play the four-step process until responses become automatic. Time your pauses, practice your questions, and rehearse value reinforcement statements.

2

Preparation Phase

For each prospect, prepare your ROI calculation framework before pricing discussions. Know their problem cost, solution value, and timeline urgency.

3

Application Phase

Use every price objection as intelligence gathering. What comparison standard are they using? What value gaps need addressing? What urgency factors can you reinforce?

4

Refinement Phase

Track which responses generate the best outcomes in your specific consulting niche. Adapt language and frameworks based on what resonates with your target market.

Remember: if you never lose a prospect due to pricing, or get comments about being expensive, you're probably leaving significant money on the table[9]. The right price objection handling doesn't eliminate all pricing resistance—it converts the right resistance into profitable relationships while maintaining your market positioning.

After seventeen years and hundreds of successful negotiations, I can tell you definitively: price objections are opportunities to demonstrate expertise, reinforce value, and ultimately close at premium rates. Master these techniques, and "too expensive" becomes the beginning of your most profitable conversations.
