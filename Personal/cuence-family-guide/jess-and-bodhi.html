Jess & Bodhi's Cuenca

Yoga, parks, parent meetups, and daily life with a toddler

The Daily Rhythm

  • Morning yoga at OM Healing Center — $5 drop-in, Ashtanga Mysore, 5 minutes from El Vergel
  • Toddler mornings at Parque de la Madre — playgrounds, running track, free planetarium
  • Weekend Expat Parents meetup at the park — organized by Cris Mino, 822-member FB group
  • Tomebamba riverwalk steps from the doorstep — stroller-friendly, colonial bridges, street art
  • CEDEI preschool nearby — one English + one Ecuadorian teacher per classroom

The Daily Rhythm

Picture a Tuesday in El Vergel. Bodhi wakes with the light — the neighborhood is quiet, residential, lined with trees that filter the Andean morning sun. Jon's already at his desk by 9am. Jess loads Bodhi into the stroller and walks five minutes to OM Healing Center to drop into an Ashtanga Mysore class for $5. The teacher Christopher trained in India multiple times; the studio also has Nicole, a Cuencana who studied in Rishikesh, teaching daily classes and sound healing. The practice is real.

After yoga, it's a 10-minute walk to Parque de la Madre — the gravitational center of Jess and Bodhi's world. The park has multiple wooden play structures across three separate play areas, a running track for laps while Bodhi runs ahead, and a free planetarium. On weekdays, Tai Chi meets at 7:30am on the grass ($2). On Saturdays, Shakti Yoga sets up free community classes right in the park. Also on Saturdays: the Expat Parents meetup, where Cris Mino gathers families with toddlers and young children for an informal morning in the sun.

By noon, Jon wraps his morning work block. Lunch is a $5 almuerzo at a nearby neighborhood restaurant — soup, a main, and juice. Afternoons: the Tomebamba riverwalk, a stroller-friendly path that runs along the river past colonial houses, stone bridges, and murals. It's nature without a car. Cuenca's four rivers create an interconnected greenway — one cyclist covered 20 miles of connected trails without touching a road. For a toddler and a parent who needs to move their body every day, this city is built for it.

Yoga & Wellness

Cuenca's yoga scene is surprisingly strong for a city its size — and priced for daily practice, not occasional splurges. The studios cluster in and around El Vergel and the Centro, so class is always a short walk away.

  • OM Healing Center $5 drop-in
    The top recommendation for Jess. Located in the Huayna-Capac neighborhood, adjacent to El Vergel. Christopher Alvarez leads with a 500-hour certification in Hatha, Ashtanga Vinyasa, and Ashtanga Mysore — trained multiple times in India. Nicole (Cuencana, trained in Rishikesh) teaches daily classes, sound healing, and Ayurvedic massage. Hugo, a York University-trained psychotherapist, is also on staff. Breathwork, homeopathy, retreats, 200-hour YTT. Retreat center in Yunguilla Valley (~1 hour south). No dedicated Ashtanga shala exists in Cuenca — OM is the closest thing.
  • Shakti Yoga $4 drop-in Free Saturdays
    The most devotional studio in Cuenca — live-in devotee teachers, primarily Spanish-language. Saturday mornings: free community yoga at Parque de la Madre. Sunday mornings: free community yoga at Parque del Paraíso. The price is right and the energy is genuine.
  • Hands Om Ayurveda Francisco Moscoso 1-36
    Abhyanga (warm oil massage), Shirodhara (oil pour on the forehead), Reiki, prenatal massage. Bilingual. Rated 5/5 on TripAdvisor. This is where you go to be genuinely restored. For context: Selina's rooftop spa does a 60-minute deep tissue massage for $17.
  • SHALA Pilates — Aubree Jeanne's yoga classes
    Tuesdays & Thursdays 6:30–7:30pm, Saturdays 8–9am at Puertas del Sol. First Saturday of each month: yoga in the park, 8–9am. Also leading a 200-hour YTT starting March 2026.
  • Windhorse Café Calle Larga $2–5 donation
    Iyengar-style classes with John Heck, Thursdays and Saturdays 9:30am. Donation-based — pay what you can.
  • Make Make Yoga
    Cuenca's only heated yoga room, for days when you want to sweat at altitude.

Most studios run classes in the 6:30–9am and 5:30–8pm windows — both work cleanly around Jon's 9-to-5 schedule. The Selina yoga deck overlooks the Tomebamba river ($15, but schedule is inconsistent). Sound healing, breathwork, regression therapy, and ecstatic dance events run through OM and Shakti — periodically organized via Facebook and WhatsApp rather than fixed weekly schedules.

Finding Mom Friends

This is the most important and most honestly complicated section. Here's what the research actually shows.

The "Expat Parents in Cuenca" Facebook group has 822 members and is actively managed by Lori Zabroski and Alicia Johnson Erickson. In a single month of scrolling (February 2026), 10–12 distinct families with children under 6 were visible, with 3–5 new families arriving or scouting per month. The group runs weekly Saturday meetups at Parque de la Madre, organized by Cris Mino — he is the first person Jess should message when she arrives. Sarah Gallagher posted recently asking who's seeking playdates in the area — she is someone to DM on day one.

Kids' creative workshops run regularly through the group: painting afternoons ($10 including materials, organized by Alejandra Vallejo Chávez), origami workshops, watercolor, ceramic painting, Mini Chefs Brunch. This is real infrastructure — not just a list of names, but recurring events you can show up to.

The Honest Signal

Liss Bermeo posted in the Cuenca parents group after living there almost a year saying she "tried to join some mommy groups here for playdates and to make new friends, but haven't had any success." Brittany Pade-Brown confirmed groups "shift because kids grow up" and couldn't recommend a current one.

This is real. The under-5 parent scene in Cuenca is thin enough that structured toddler playgroups don't always sustain themselves. The Saturday meetups give you a starting point, but you will likely need to build the connections yourself rather than joining an already-humming social circle.

The critical comparison: this weakness is visible and documented in Cuenca. In Oaxaca, the equivalent data simply doesn't exist — no dedicated parents group, no organized meetups, no visible arrival patterns. For Jess arriving cold, Cuenca's organizational layer means she can find the Saturday park meetup on day three. Oaxaca would require weeks of informal WhatsApp networking to find the equivalent. Either city probably has 15–30 active families with children under 5. Cuenca makes those 15–30 people findable.

CEDEI Preschool

CEDEI (Centro de Educación Continua para el Idioma Inglés) is the standard first recommendation for expat families looking at early childhood education in Cuenca. It's a not-for-profit bilingual school with one English-speaking teacher and one Ecuadorian teacher per classroom — a structure that gives Bodhi both language exposure and cultural grounding simultaneously.

CEDEI runs from preschool through high school, so there's no school change required if you stay long-term. It sits near El Vergel, which is a core reason the neighborhood wins: you can walk Bodhi to school, then walk to OM for yoga, then walk to the park — no car required, no logistics overhead.

For broader context: Cuenca has 8–9 schools actively used by expat families (compared to 5–6 in Oaxaca), with firsthand parent reviews across 6+ schools in a single month of the Facebook group. CEDEI is the most-mentioned for expat early childhood, but LunaSol Waldorf (founded 2019), Colegio Alemán Stiehle, Santana, and Bell Academy are all discussed with real parent experiences. Preschool costs range from $90–250/month at most of these schools — dramatically lower than Cape Town or comparable cities.

Finding a Nanny

Finding a part-time nanny in Cuenca is realistic and well within budget. The channels:

  • "Expat Parents in Cuenca" Facebook group — Post a description of what you need. Parents share recommendations and sometimes their own nannies when circumstances change.
  • GringoPost and YapaTree classifieds — Daily and weekly English-language listings. Post or browse.
  • Language school students — Advanced English students at Universidad de Cuenca and Universidad del Azuay studying English or translation are an excellent pipeline. They want to practice; you want bilingual childcare.
  • School parent networks — Once Bodhi is enrolled at CEDEI, the parent community becomes a referral source.

Budget: $400–600/month part-time, $600–900/month full-time. The bilingual requirement is significantly easier to fill than in Oaxaca — Cuenca's larger English-speaking expat orbit creates a deeper pool of bilingual Ecuadorians who've had sustained exposure to English speakers. This is a structural advantage, not just a lucky coincidence.

The Tomebamba Riverwalk

The Tomebamba River runs along the southern edge of El Vergel and the historic center, and the riverwalk along its banks is one of Cuenca's great daily gifts. From the El Vergel apartment, it's steps to the path — no driving, no planning, just out the door and into nature.

The walk runs past colonial houses perched above the riverbank (the famous Barranco), the Puente Roto (the "broken bridge," an 1870s iron bridge that floods took out, now an iconic photo spot), and a string of murals and street art. Bridges connect the path at regular intervals. It is entirely stroller-friendly on the riverside path — flat, paved, shaded in parts.

Cuenca has four rivers total — Tomebamba, Yanuncay, Tarqui, Machángara — and they form an interconnected greenway system. The Jardín Botánico de Cuenca spans 21 hectares along the Yanuncay and Tarqui rivers with 8,000 plant species, flat stroller-friendly paths, footbridges, and educational placards — free admission, excellent for toddler walks when you want something different. A cyclist once reported covering ~20 miles of connected river trails without touching a road. For Jess and Bodhi, the basic requirement is simpler: daily movement, outside, without a car. The Tomebamba delivers that from the doorstep.

"Parque de la Madre is the hub — Saturday parent meetups, weekday Tai Chi at 7:30am, Frisbee Fridays at noon, and free Shakti Yoga on Saturday mornings. If you're a new family in El Vergel, this park is your starting point." — Expat Parents in Cuenca Facebook group

The Bottom Line for Jess

Cuenca is not a city that has cracked the code on toddler social life. The under-5 expat parent scene is real but thin — probably 15–30 active families, not a hundred. What it has that no comparable city in Latin America can match is organizational infrastructure: a dedicated 822-member Facebook group, weekly meetups with a named coordinator, visible new family arrivals every month, and a neighborhood (El Vergel) where the yoga studio, the preschool, the park, the riverwalk, and the parent meetup are all within a 15-minute walk of each other.

Jess will likely need to be a little entrepreneurial about building her mom circle — show up to the Saturday meetup, DM Sarah Gallagher, post in the group asking who else has a toddler. The infrastructure exists to make those connections happen quickly. What it won't do is hand her a pre-formed social circle. But that's true of every city in this category, and Cuenca at least makes the path legible.
