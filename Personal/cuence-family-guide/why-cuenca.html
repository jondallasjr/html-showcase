Reason #2

USD Currency

Ecuador adopted the US dollar in 2000. This is not a trivial detail. It means no conversion math at the market, no watching exchange rates, no mental division at every transaction. Your income arrives in USD. Your spending happens in USD. The number on the price tag is the number you pay.

Compare that to Mexico: roughly 18 pesos per dollar. You spend your first year doing mental division at every single transaction. Is that 280-peso dinner expensive or cheap? You have to calculate. Every time. For years. In Cuenca, a $7 dinner is a $7 dinner.

"No currency conversion, no exchange rate to watch, no mental math at the market. Your income arrives in USD, your spending happens in USD. Cash is still king for smaller transactions." — Oaxaca vs. Cuenca Practical Guide, March 2026

Practical note: ATMs dispense $20 bills. Change can be tight at smaller vendors — the Central Bank has change machines in central Cuenca. Schwab debit card reimburses all ATM fees globally, which is the expat standard. Remitly works well for transfers in; budget for a 5% exit tax on outbound transfers over $1,200.

Reason #3

Safety

Cuenca was rated the #1 safest city in South America in 2025. That's not tourism-board language — it's a meaningful data point in a region where safety is a real and variable concern.

For daily life this means: low police friction, no mordida (bribe) culture to navigate, and a city where you can walk home at night without running a mental risk calculation. Ecuador's police bribery culture exists but is not pervasive in Cuenca city life. Most encounters end without incident.

This matters especially with a toddler. Jess walking Bodhi to the park, taking a taxi alone at night, leaving the apartment with just a stroller — these should all be unremarkable. In Cuenca, they are.

Reason #6

The Visa Pathway

Ecuador's digital nomad visa — the VERHU — explicitly covers remote workers earning foreign income. Jon qualifies directly. Jess joins as a dependent.

The pathway is clean: digital nomad visa (2 years, renewable) → temporary residency → permanent residency after 21 months of continuous legal status. No minimum investment required.

The Jess factor: Ecuador is notably Zimbabwe-passport friendly. Jess can enter visa-free for 90 days, which is rare in Latin America and eliminates the consular visa headaches required for Mexico, Costa Rica, and Colombia. This is a meaningful practical advantage that directly affects how you can plan your arrival and first months.

The bureaucracy is slower than Mexico's, but the legal framework is cleaner. Find a bilingual immigration lawyer in Cuenca — several specialize in VERHU applications — and budget 6–8 weeks for processing.

Honest Assessment

The Honest Tradeoffs

This is a real guide, not a tourism brochure. Cuenca is the right choice for your family — but knowing what you're trading for what makes the choice sharper and the arrival smoother.

What you're trading for what you're getting

  • Altitude (8,400ft). First 3–7 days: expect headaches, fatigue, shortness of breath, disrupted sleep. Hydrate aggressively, go slow the first week. For Bodhi specifically: monitor for unusual irritability or labored breathing. This isn't La Paz — it's manageable — but the adjustment is real and not optional.
  • Cooler climate, always. Days are 65–72°F. Evenings drop to 50–55°F. Layers every day, always. A light jacket every morning, a warmer layer every evening. The "eternal spring" reputation is accurate — but it also means you're never in a t-shirt and sandals. If you loved the warmth of Chiang Mai, Cuenca is the opposite of that.
  • Retiree-heavy expat scene. The dominant demographic is 55+, often American or Canadian. The younger nomad layer is building (thanks partly to VERHU), but you'll notice the age skew. The cultural programming skews toward it too. You'll find your people, but you'll be swimming in a larger pond of people who are at a different life stage.
  • Thin under-5 parent scene. The organized infrastructure exists and is better than Oaxaca's. But in absolute terms, the active under-5 parent scene is maybe 15–30 families. One parent noted that after almost a year in Cuenca, she still hadn't found a toddler playgroup that stuck. The weekly Parque de la Madre meetup is your best shot at finding a consistent circle quickly — but expect to work for it.
  • Comfort versus aliveness. This is the most honest tradeoff. Cuenca is the lower-friction city: no currency math, reliable internet, easier logistics, less police friction, lower rent. But as the research notes: "comfort can become its own kind of trap when what you actually want is to feel alive." The city is beautiful and organized and livable. It's not wild. It doesn't have Oaxaca's magnetic energy or Chiang Mai's chaos. If what you need right now is a stable base that works — Cuenca is that. If what you need is to feel like you're living at the edge of something — that's a different conversation.
"The single biggest practical risk in Cuenca is the one you've already identified: it's the safer, easier, more comfortable choice — and comfort can become its own kind of trap when what you actually want is to feel alive." — Oaxaca vs. Cuenca Practical Guide, March 2026

And yet: the timezone alone makes this right. Jon working 9–5 in Chiang Mai meant no family. In Cuenca, family life is structurally possible in a way it wasn't before. That's not a small thing. That's the whole thing.

The bottom line

Cuenca is the lower-friction city for the mechanics of daily life: no currency math, reliable internet, bilingual nanny access, lower rent, no police/bribe friction, and a clean visa path for a Zimbabwean passport. The timezone makes family life structurally possible. Those facts, combined, are why this is the choice.

Go in clear-eyed about the altitude, the cool climate, the retiree demographic, and the thin under-5 parent scene. None of those are dealbreakers. The timezone is a dealbreaker in the other direction — it's why you're going.
