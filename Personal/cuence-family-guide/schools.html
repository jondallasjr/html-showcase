Schools & Childcare

8–9 school options, bilingual preschool from $90/month, and a homeschooling community that's genuinely big.

The Short Version

  • CEDEI is the standard expat pick — bilingual, not-for-profit, near El Vergel, preschool through high school.
  • LunaSol Waldorf exists and has expat families enrolled, but it's young (founded 2019) and still establishing.
  • Private preschool runs $90–250/month. Santana-level schools run ~$450/month.
  • There is a big homeschooling community in Cuenca — a real option for year one.
  • Nannies run $400–600/month part-time, $600–900 full-time. Bilingual nannies are easier to find here than in Oaxaca.

CEDEI School — The Default Choice

Top Expat Recommendation

CEDEI School

Bilingual Not-for-profit Preschool–High School Near El Vergel

CEDEI is the standard expat recommendation and the school that comes up first in every parent forum thread. It's bilingual and not-for-profit — unusual in a market full of for-profit private schools. Each classroom has one English-speaking teacher and one Ecuadorian teacher working together, so kids get genuine bilingual immersion rather than a token English class twice a week.

The location matters for us: CEDEI is near El Vergel, which is the neighborhood we're most likely to land in. That means Bodhi could potentially walk or bike. It runs preschool through high school, so there's no forced transition at age 6 or 12 — you pick the school once.

This is our default assumption for Bodhi. It's the place to look at first. The parent community at CEDEI is expat-active, which means it's also a social entry point for Jess.

LunaSol Waldorf — The Alternative

Waldorf Initiative

CENEDIS LunaSol

Waldorf pedagogy Founded October 2019 Preschool + Primary Expat families enrolled

If Waldorf pedagogy is something we want to explore, LunaSol exists and is real. It was founded in October 2019, which makes it young — just six years old, compared to Oaxaca's Papalotes Waldorf which has been running since 2009. Confirmed expat families are enrolled and visible in the Facebook parents group. It's not a rumor or a waiting-list-only situation.

The honest assessment: LunaSol is still establishing itself. Small school, newer staff culture, fewer years of track record. If the Waldorf approach is central to how we want to raise Bodhi, it's worth a visit. If we're Waldorf-curious but not committed, CEDEI is the safer bet with a more established community around it.

These two schools — CEDEI and LunaSol — are the ones to visit in person during any scouting trip.

Homeschooling

Multiple parents in the Cuenca expat group have described the homeschooling community as "big." This isn't a fringe option — it's an established track with enough families that it has its own social infrastructure. For a first year in Cuenca, when we're still figuring out neighborhoods, routines, and which school is the right fit for Bodhi, homeschooling could be a smart bridge.

The homeschooling community in Cuenca overlaps heavily with the LunaSol and Montessori parent networks — the alternative education crowd tends to cluster. The Expat Parents in Cuenca Facebook group (822 members) is the right place to find current contacts and active co-ops.

Ecuador's legal framework for homeschooling is worth researching directly, as it has evolved in recent years. The expat parents group will have current firsthand information from families navigating it.

Cost Comparison

$90 Preschool, low end
$250 Preschool, high end
~$450 Santana / mid-tier
~$210+ Oaxaca baseline

Private preschool in Cuenca runs $90–250/month depending on the school. The mid-tier schools (Santana, Jefferson, Bell Academy) run around $450/month. The premium German-Ecuadorian and American-curriculum schools run higher.

For comparison: the baseline private school in Oaxaca (Teizcali, which is well-regarded and popular with expats) runs $210+/month. Cuenca's low end is cheaper, and its options at every price tier are wider.

This is one of the clearest financial wins of Cuenca over Oaxaca for a family with a young child. You get more choices, at lower prices, with better English-language infrastructure around them.

Finding a Nanny

Bilingual nannies are more findable in Cuenca than in Oaxaca, where English fluency is rarer among childcare workers. The channels that work:

  • Expat Parents in Cuenca Facebook group — post directly, ask for referrals. This is the primary channel. Parents actively share recommendations and warnings.
  • YapaTree / GringoPost — both have classified sections. GringoPost has 45,000+ subscribers; YapaTree is the newer, more curated service. Post a wanted ad in both.
  • Language school student networks — university students studying English often want part-time work with expat families. Mutually beneficial: they practice English, you get affordable help. Ask at CEDEI or local language schools for referrals.
  • LunaSol and Montessori parent networks — alternative school parents share childcare workers. If you're visiting those schools, ask parents directly while you're there.
  • Word of mouth at El Vergel parks — the playground and park scene in El Vergel is where expat parents with young children naturally cluster. Showing up regularly builds the network that leads to nanny referrals.

Kids Activities

Beyond school, Cuenca has a real activity scene for young children — much more organized and findable than what you'd encounter arriving cold in Oaxaca.

Creative Workshops

Painting, origami, watercolor, ceramics, and cooking workshops for kids are regularly organized by Alejandra Vallejo Chávez and local studios. These surface in the Expat Parents Facebook group and the YEES events calendar. They're the kind of thing Jess can find on week two, not month six.

Museo Pumapungo — Best Toddler Museum

This is the standout. Pumapungo is a free museum built on actual Inca ruins, with live llamas on the grounds and a bird rescue sanctuary. For a toddler, it hits every note: big open space, animals, ancient stones to scramble on, things to look at. Multiple expat parents have called it the best toddler destination in the city.

It's run by Ecuador's Ministry of Culture, which keeps it free and well-maintained. The combination of living animals, bird rescue, and Inca archaeology is genuinely unusual — there's nothing quite like it in any of the other cities we considered.

"Pumapungo — free admission, Inca ruins, live llamas on the grounds, and a bird rescue sanctuary. The best toddler museum in Cuenca." — From our research

Parque de la Madre

The Expat Parents group holds weekly meetups at Parque de la Madre, coordinated by Cris Mino. This is the most reliable recurring social event for young families in the city. Confirmed ongoing as of early 2026. Show up on week one — it's the fastest path to meeting other parents with kids Bodhi's age.

Biblioteca de Cuenca (Universidad de Cuenca)

Public access, 63,000+ volumes. Not a children's library per se, but worth knowing exists. The university campus itself is beautiful and walkable — a good place for a stroller loop on a sunny afternoon.
