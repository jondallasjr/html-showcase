Cajas National Park

Thirty minutes from your front door is one of the most otherworldly landscapes in South America. Parque Nacional Cajas sits at 3,100–4,450 meters elevation — páramo grasslands, ancient Polylepis forests, and 786 bodies of water carved by glaciers. The light changes every few minutes. Nothing looks like anywhere else.

The main entry point is the visitor center at Laguna Toreadora — the Toreadora Loop (2.2 km, boardwalks, easy grade) and Laguna Llaviucu loop (1.7 km, lower elevation) are both doable with Bodhi in a carrier. Entry is free or minimal cost. A bus from Terminal Terrestre runs for $2–3.

"The altitude at the visitor center is 3,850m — acclimatize first in Cuenca before tackling Cajas. Once you're adjusted, it's an easy day trip with a toddler." — From our research

The Polylepis forests — gnarled, ancient trees at high altitude — are rare ecosystems found only in the Andes. They predate the Spanish conquest. On a clear day the views across the lakes are extraordinary; on a foggy day the place feels genuinely mythic. Either way, it earns the drive.

Tomebamba Riverwalk

This is the daily nature fix — no car required. The Tomebamba riverwalk runs along the northern edge of El Vergel, beneath the colonial Barranco houses that perch dramatically above the river. Paths below, painted facades above, bridges connecting across to El Centro.

Jess and Bodhi's Morning Walk

The riverwalk is stroller-friendly, flat, and connects to Pumapungo, the Puente Roto, and street art along the banks. This is what a good morning looks like in Cuenca — walk out the front door and follow the river.

Cuenca's four rivers — Tomebamba, Yanuncay, Tarqui, Machángara — create an interconnected greenway system. One cyclist reported covering ~20 miles of connected trails without touching a road. The Parque Lineal Yanuncay is the most popular walking route: tree-lined, benches along the river, unhurried.

Jardín Botánico

The new Jardín Botánico de Cuenca spans 21 hectares along the Yanuncay and Tarqui rivers, housing 8,000 plant species from across Ecuador and the wider Andes. It's free, flat, stroller-friendly, and genuinely impressive — not a dusty botanical museum but a living landscape with footbridges, educational placards, and mountain views from the elevated boardwalk.

Free and stroller-ready

Free admission. Flat paths throughout. Elevated boardwalk with panoramic views of the city and mountains. Educational signage in Spanish and English. One of the best reasons to settle on the Yanuncay side of the city.

Parque El Paraíso

The largest park in Cuenca. El Paraíso has it all: forest trails along a river, multiple playgrounds, a lake with paddleboats, a children's traffic school with toy cars, and — on Sunday mornings — free community yoga with Shakti. It's the kind of park that justifies choosing a city.

Hiking Groups

Cuenca has an organized community for getting beyond the city. You don't need to figure out logistics alone.

Club Sangay

Organized monthly hikes ranging from easy day trips to multi-day Andean routes. Long-running group with consistent scheduling.

Fernando Quito Hurtado

Organizes multiple hikes per month through the YEES network. More frequent schedule than Club Sangay — good for getting out regularly.

Both groups are accessible through the expat community channels — GringoPost, YapaTree, and the Cuenca Circle of Friends Facebook group post upcoming hikes. The hiking scene is active enough that you can be on a trail most weekends without planning far ahead.

Weekend Trips

Cuenca's geographic position in the central Andes gives you coast, highlands, and jungle all within reach. Ecuador is physically small — the distances that would take a week in other countries take an afternoon here.
