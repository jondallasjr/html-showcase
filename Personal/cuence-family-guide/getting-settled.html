Getting There & Settled

Flights, first week, apartment hunting, and setting up life

The Quick Version

  • No direct flights from Cape Town — route through the US (Dallas/Houston) or Europe
  • Cuenca's airport (CUE) is small; most international connections go through Quito or Guayaquil
  • First week: go slow, hydrate, watch Bodhi at altitude (8,400 ft)
  • Find an apartment via YapaTree or GringoPost — target El Vergel, $400–700/month
  • SIM card: Claro or Movistar, available same day on arrival
  • ATMs: Schwab debit card reimburses all fees — the expat standard

Getting There from Cape Town

There are no direct flights from Cape Town (CPT) to anywhere in Ecuador. Every route involves at least one connection, and often two. The two main options are routing through the United States or through Europe.

Route Option 1 — Through the US (Recommended)

Cape Town → Dallas/Fort Worth or Houston → Quito or Guayaquil → Cuenca. American Airlines and United fly the South Africa–Texas corridor. From DFW or IAH, you can connect to Quito (UIO) or Guayaquil (GYE) and then take a short domestic hop or bus to Cuenca.

Total travel time: approximately 22–28 hours door-to-door depending on layover timing. Not pleasant, but no worse than Cape Town to Southeast Asia. Booking the segments separately often saves significant money — watch CPT → DFW and DFW → UIO independently.

Route Option 2 — Through Europe

Cape Town → Amsterdam/Madrid/London → Quito or Guayaquil → Cuenca. KLM, Iberia, and British Airways fly this corridor. LATAM operates Quito and Guayaquil routes from several European hubs. Total travel time is similar — 24–30 hours — but can offer better fares in certain windows, especially through Madrid with Iberia.

Cuenca Airport (CUE)

Mariscal Lamar Airport is small, compact, and refreshingly easy to navigate. It sits inside the city — you're in the historic center within 10 minutes of landing. However, international options are extremely limited: primarily connections to Quito (20–40 min flight) and Guayaquil (45 min). Some seasonal LATAM routes exist but schedules change. Plan to enter Ecuador through Quito or Guayaquil and connect to Cuenca.

On arrival in Quito or Guayaquil: Both airports have domestic connections to Cuenca throughout the day. LATAM and Avianca operate these routes. If you miss the connection or prefer ground travel, Quito to Cuenca by bus is about 8 hours via the Pan-American Highway — long but scenic and cheap (~$12). Guayaquil to Cuenca by bus is about 3.5–4 hours. Buses are comfortable and reliable.

Good news for the Dallas side of the family: this is a genuinely reasonable trip. No 24-hour marathons. No absurd stopovers. Cuenca is well-connected to Texas.

The First Week

Cuenca sits at 8,400 feet (2,560 meters). That's not Cusco or La Paz — but it's real altitude, and it will knock you back if you don't respect it. The first 3–7 days are the adjustment window. This is not negotiable.

Altitude Adjustment Plan

Expect headaches, fatigue, shortness of breath on exertion, and disrupted sleep in the first few days. Some people sail through it; some don't. The variables are individual physiology and how busy you try to be. The fix is the same regardless: slow down, hydrate aggressively, skip alcohol for the first week, and don't plan anything demanding.

Watch Bodhi carefully. Children sometimes show altitude effects differently — unusual irritability, loss of appetite, or labored breathing are the signals to watch for. None of these should be alarming on their own (all are common in adults too), but if breathing is labored or appetite completely disappears for more than a day, descend to lower altitude and consult a doctor. Cuenca is manageable altitude for a toddler, but monitor.

Week 1 Daily Rhythm (Gentle Version)

This is not a week for productivity or aggressive exploration. It's a week for landing.

  • Days 1–2: Unpack, sleep, eat simple food, walk slowly around your immediate block. Let the body adjust. Drink water constantly.
  • Days 3–4: Walk the neighborhood. Find El Vergel's rhythm. Walk south to Parque de la Madre — it's 10 minutes on foot from most El Vergel apartments. Don't rush.
  • Days 5–6: Walk the Tomebamba riverwalk. It runs east-west along the river below the Barranco and is one of the most beautiful urban walks in South America. Flat, stroller-friendly, and impossible to get lost on.
  • Day 7: Saturday. Go to the Expat Parents meetup at Parque de la Madre. This is the single highest-value move in the first week. The group posts the time on the Expat Parents in Cuenca Facebook page — typically weekend mornings.

Don't Skip the Saturday Parent Meetup

The Expat Parents in Cuenca Facebook group (822 members, coordinators Lori Zabroski and Alicia Johnson Erickson) runs a weekly Saturday meetup at Parque de la Madre. In any given month, 10–12 active families with children under 6 show up. This is Jess's fastest path into community — and for Bodhi, the fastest path to other kids who speak English and won't stare at him like he's from another planet.

You can go on day one if you're feeling okay. The group is used to fresh arrivals introducing themselves. Show up, say you just landed, and you'll have three people recommending their pediatrician, nanny, and favorite grocery store before you've sat down.

Finding an Apartment

Cuenca has a well-developed English-language housing infrastructure built specifically for the expat community. This is one of its clearest advantages over most Latin American cities — you don't have to know anyone or navigate Spanish-only classifieds to find a furnished apartment.

What to Target

El Vergel, $400–700/month for a furnished 2BR. This is the target. It puts you walking distance from Parque de la Madre, OM Healing Center, CEDEI, the Tomebamba riverwalk, and Supermaxi. Post-COVID the supply of furnished places is tighter than it was pre-2020, so start browsing before you arrive and reach out to landlords with an intro message explaining your situation.

Setting Up

SIM Card — Day One

Get a SIM card at the airport or at any Claro or Movistar store in the city. Both are reliable in Cuenca. Claro has broader network coverage nationally; Movistar is comparable in the city. A prepaid SIM with a data plan costs $5–15 and takes about 10 minutes with a passport. You'll want this before you leave the airport. For home internet, confirm fiber availability before signing any lease — but in El Vergel, it's not a concern.

Money and ATMs

Ecuador uses the US dollar. No mental math, no exchange rate anxiety, no confusion at the market. Your income arrives in USD, your spending happens in USD. This alone eliminates a significant layer of daily friction compared to any peso-based country.

ATMs charge fees: Banco Guayaquil runs about $3.51 per withdrawal, Produbanco/Banco del Pacífico about $4.60. JEP credit union ATMs are cheapest at $0.50. The Schwab debit card reimburses all ATM fees globally — it's the expat standard and the card to use. Remitly is recommended over Wise for transfers into Ecuador (Wise has intermediary bank friction on some routes).

One practical note: ATMs dispense $20 bills, and change can be difficult at small vendors. The Central Bank maintains change machines in central Cuenca locations. Keep smaller bills when you can.

Paying Bills — Servipagos

Servipagos, a Produbanco division, handles most utilities in-person. Bring cash, the biller name, and your account number. It's not as seamless as OXXO in Mexico, but it works. Most utilities also have mobile apps once you have a local bank account — electricity via the Centro Sur app, water via ETAPA. You won't need a local bank account urgently for the first month; Schwab covers daily spending and Remitly handles transfers.

Health Insurance

On the digital nomad visa, you're required to show health coverage. International plans (SafetyWing, Cigna Global, Allianz) work. Budget $100–200/month for the family. Private healthcare in Cuenca is genuinely good and cheap — a specialist consultation runs $30–60. Hospital Metropolitano and Monte Sinai are the main private hospitals; both have English-speaking staff in key departments.

The Chiang Mai Question

Your stuff is in storage in Chiang Mai. Thailand to Ecuador is not the most convenient shipping corridor in the world. Here's the realistic picture.

Option 1 — Ship Everything (Sea Freight)

International sea freight from Chiang Mai to Guayaquil (Ecuador's main port) takes 6–10 weeks and costs $2,000–5,000+ depending on volume, depending on whether you're shipping a shared container (LCL) or full container (FCL). You'd need a Chiang Mai freight forwarder to consolidate and ship to Guayaquil, where an Ecuadorian customs broker handles clearance and delivery to Cuenca. Ecuador's import duties on household goods under the "menaje de casa" exemption for new residents are reduced but require paperwork tied to your residency visa. This is the right option if you have significant furniture, appliances, or items that can't be replaced cheaply in Cuenca.

Option 2 — Ship the Essentials, Replace the Rest

Given that furnished apartments in Cuenca cost $400–700/month and already include furniture, it may make more financial sense to ship only genuinely irreplaceable items (art, sentimental objects, specialized gear, quality clothing) as excess baggage or via a courier service, and either sell or donate the furniture in Chiang Mai. A full air freight shipment of 50–100kg costs roughly $500–1,500 and arrives in 5–10 days — dramatically faster than sea freight and potentially cheaper when you factor in the duties and broker fees on a container.

Option 3 — Keep Chiang Mai Storage, Decide Later

Storage in Chiang Mai is cheap ($50–150/month for a significant unit). If you're not sure what you'll want after the first few months in Cuenca, keeping things in storage while you settle in is a legitimate strategy. Once you have an apartment and know what it lacks, you can make an informed decision about what's worth shipping and what isn't.

Practical note on customs: Ecuador's customs process for international household goods requires documentation that ties the shipment to a new resident's arrival. Coordinate with an Ecuadorian customs broker early — several specialize in expat moves and have relationships with the relevant agencies. Ask in the GringoPost community for recommendations; this comes up frequently.

First Month Priorities — Week by Week

Don't try to do everything at once. The first month in a new country is about establishing rhythm, not checking boxes. Here's a framework.

Week 1 — Land

  • Rest and altitude adjustment
  • Get SIM card
  • Walk El Vergel, find your grocery store
  • Riverwalk at your own pace
  • Saturday parent meetup at Parque de la Madre
  • Watch Bodhi for altitude signs

Week 2 — Explore

  • Find your yoga studio (try OM Healing Center first)
  • Establish a grocery routine (Supermaxi + Mercado 10 de Agosto)
  • Return to Saturday parent meetup
  • Walk south to Jardín Botánico
  • Sign up for YapaTree's weekly email
  • Find one café as your base

Week 3 — Root

  • Visit school options (CEDEI, LunaSol Waldorf)
  • Start nanny search via Expat Parents FB group
  • Try one cultural event (IdiomArte or La Guarida)
  • Day trip to Baños for thermal baths
  • Nail down apartment if still on short-term

Week 4 — Engage

  • Deeper community engagement
  • Day trip to Cajas National Park
  • Attend one evening event (jazz, symphony, or storytelling)
  • Look into Spanish classes
  • Cuenca Coworking Club meetup for Jon
  • Make a decision on Chiang Mai storage
