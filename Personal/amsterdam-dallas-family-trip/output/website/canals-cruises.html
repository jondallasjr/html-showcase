Canals & Cruises

165 canals. One city. See it from the water.

Amsterdam's grachtengordel is a UNESCO World Heritage site: 400-year-old canals you can cruise in 75 minutes, cycle along all afternoon, or simply sit beside. Bodhi will sleep through at least one of these.

Canal Cruises

One cruise is essential. Two is better: once in the afternoon, once at dusk when the light hits the gabled facades. Avoid 11AM–3PM crowds; the boats fill up and the light is flat.

City Card: Stromma cruises included free

Each I amsterdam City Card includes Stromma canal cruises at no extra charge. Activate the card on Day 4 morning and board without queuing at the dock. This is the best value for the family: no separate booking, no fee.

Recommended City Card Bodhi free (under 4)

What the cruise covers

60–75 minutes, fully seated
Covered boats available
Stroller folds into boat storage

The standard route covers Prinsengracht, Keizersgracht, Herengracht, the Amstel river, and Jordaan canals, passing the Anne Frank House, Westerkerk tower, and Amsterdam's famously narrow canal houses. English audio commentary throughout.

The motion is gentle enough that Bodhi may well fall asleep. Bring layers; it can feel cool on the water even on warm late-May afternoons.

Go twice — afternoon and dusk

Golden hour ends around 9:30PM in late May. An evening cruise at 7–8PM shows a different city: softer light on the facades, fewer tourists on the bridges, long amber reflections in the water. The afternoon cruise is the orientation; the evening cruise is the memory.

The Canal Ring

Understanding what you're looking at makes the cruise more interesting. Three minutes of context.

UNESCO heritage

Amsterdam's grachtengordel (canal ring) was planned and dug in the early 1600s as one of the world's first deliberate urban expansions: three concentric semicircles radiating from the medieval city center. It took 50 years to build and was, at the time, the largest urban engineering project in Europe.

The three main canals are named for their original patrons: Herengracht (Lords' Canal), Keizersgracht (Emperor's Canal), and Prinsengracht (Prince's Canal). The merchant families who built the narrow, tall canal houses were showing off. Amsterdam in 1650 was the wealthiest city on earth, and the houses proved it.

The "Golden Bend" on Herengracht — between Leidsestraat and Vijzelstraat — is where the richest merchants built the widest houses. The single most beautiful stretch of canal in the city.
Best spots along the canal ring
  • Herengracht, Golden Bend — widest, grandest canal houses in Amsterdam
  • Reguliersgracht, 7 Bridges viewpoint — stand at the Keizersgracht intersection and look down Reguliersgracht to see seven arched bridges in a row; Amsterdam's most photographed canal spot
  • Brouwersgracht at Prinsengracht — Jordaan neighbourhood, quieter canals, houseboats, fewer tourists
  • Prinsengracht near the Anne Frank House — the most recognizable canal-and-house view in Amsterdam
Late May in Amsterdam: flowering trees still lining the canals, golden-hour light on the water by 9PM

Cycling the Canals

The best way to understand the canal ring is to cycle it slowly, stopping at the bridges you want. A cargo bike makes this easy with Bodhi.

Family favourite Very flat

Cargo bikes (bakfietsen)

Bodhi rides in the front box with seatbelts
~€32.95/day
MacBike, Star Bikes, Rent a Bike

A cargo bike puts Bodhi in the front box, facing forward, visible at all times. It's how Amsterdam families move children around the city, and it's exactly as fun as it looks. Rental shops near the apartment include MacBike, Star Bikes, and Rent a Bike.

Tip: Amsterdam cycling traffic is intense on main roads. Stick to canal-side streets and residential routes; they're calm and well-marked.

The canal ring circuit — 8km, very flat

Suggested route
  • Start at the rental shop near the apartment
  • South along Prinsengracht past the Anne Frank House and Westerkerk
  • Cross to Amstel river — wide, scenic, worth stopping to watch the boats
  • North along Amstel to Plantage neighbourhood
  • Back via Keizersgracht — quieter, lined with linden trees
  • Stop at Reguliersgracht for the 7 bridges view
  • ~8km total, entirely flat, no major intersections if you time the crossings

Houseboats

About 2,500 houseboats float in Amsterdam's canals. Roughly 15% are permanent residences — people's actual homes, complete with gardens, solar panels, and cats on the roof.

Quick visit

Houseboat Museum

Prinsengracht 296K — near the Anne Frank House
€4.50/adult
20–30 minutes

A restored cargo vessel turned permanent floating home. Small, genuinely interesting, easy to combine with the Anne Frank House visit; it's on the same stretch of Prinsengracht. Shows exactly how compact and clever houseboat living actually is.

Best streets for houseboat-watching
  • Prinsengracht — densest concentration, especially between Westermarkt and Leidsegracht
  • Jordaan canals (Brouwersgracht, Palmgracht, Egelantiersgracht) — more residential, fewer tourists
  • Look for the ones with roof terraces and bicycles chained to the bow

Practical

Avoid 11AM–3PM on the cruise boats

The boats fill up fast in peak season. Go early (first departure ~9:30AM) or late afternoon; conditions and light are better in both slots. City Card holders can board without pre-booking at off-peak times.

For the cruise

  • City Card gets you on Stromma free — show it at the dock
  • Strollers fold and go in storage below; attendants help
  • Motion sickness is very rare; canals are calm flat water
  • Bring layers — open-sided boats catch the canal breeze
  • Golden hour in late May: roughly 8:30–9:30PM
  • Bodhi under 4 is free on every operator

For canal cycling

  • Book cargo bike rental online in advance — especially weekends
  • Stick to canal-side streets; avoid Damrak and Leidseplein
  • Amsterdam is entirely flat — no gears needed
  • Bodhi's box seat has seatbelts; test them before setting off
  • 7 bridges view: stand at Keizersgracht & Reguliersgracht intersection
